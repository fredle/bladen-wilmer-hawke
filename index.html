Lord Bladen Wilmer Hawke 9th Baron Hawke of Towton

31 DEC 1901  ·  5 JUL 1985
Strike

Overview

Bladen Wilmer Hawke, 9th Baron Hawke of Towton was a British peer, colonial trader, wartime civil servant, and Conservative Lord-in-Waiting in the early 1950s. He is remembered today less for his political office than for his namesake: the beach at Hawke's Bay, Karachi, where he kept a seaside bungalow in the 1930s. A Wykehamist and King's Scholar, he managed the Bombay-Burma Trading Corporation, served in the Ministry of Economic Warfare during the Second World War, and succeeded to the ancient barony founded by his illustrious ancestor, Admiral of the Fleet Edward Hawke, victor of Quiberon Bay.

Portraits

The National Portrait Gallery in London holds a photographic portrait of the 9th Baron catalogued under sitter reference mw224736. It is the principal surviving likeness of him in a public institution and shows him in middle life — the period during which he served in the Ministry of Economic Warfare and later as Lord-in-Waiting.

A further editorial photograph of Lord Hawke is held commercially in the Shutterstock editorial archive (image reference 4771251a). His grave, and a small biographical note on his later life, is recorded on Find a Grave under the memorial for “Sir Bladen Wilmer Hawke (1901–1985)”.

Early Life & Education

Bladen Wilmer Hawke was born on the last day of 1901, the eldest son of Edward Julian Hawke, 8th Baron Hawke (1873–1939) and his wife Frances Alice Wilmer, whom his father had married on 9 October 1900. His middle name perpetuates his mother's maiden name — a convention by which the Hawke family has long carried female lineage forward into the patronymic.

He was sent to Winchester College, one of England's oldest public schools, and from there proceeded to King's College, Cambridge, where he read for his degree. He graduated B.A. in 1923 and, some twenty-three years later, incorporated M.A. in 1946.

Commercial & Wartime Career

Bombay-Burma Trading Corporation

After Cambridge, Hawke entered the service of the Bombay-Burma Trading Corporation, the great Scottish-founded teak house established in 1863 by the Wallace Brothers of Edinburgh. By the inter-war years the Corporation was the leading exporter of Burmese teak and a powerful commercial presence from Rangoon to Bombay. Hawke rose to a managerial position, a posting which took him across British India and the Far East and which brought him, in the 1930s, to the coast west of Karachi.

Ministry of Economic Warfare & the War Office

On the outbreak of the Second World War, Hawke was recalled to government service. He served in the Ministry of Economic Warfare, the department established in September 1939 to coordinate economic operations against the Axis powers — blockade, pre-emptive purchasing, and the Special Operations Executive — and subsequently at the War Office. It was during this period, on 4 September 1939, that his father died and he succeeded to the barony.

Lord-in-Waiting, 1953–1957

Under the Conservative governments of Winston Churchill and Anthony Eden, Hawke was appointed a Lord-in-Waiting — a salaried member of the Royal Household who acts as a government whip in the House of Lords and attends the sovereign on State occasions. He held the office from 1953 to 1957, covering the period of the Queen's coronation, the Suez crisis, and Eden's resignation.

Legacy: Hawke's Bay, Karachi

The most visible legacy of Bladen Wilmer Hawke is not a parliamentary act or a title but a stretch of coastline. During his time with the Bombay-Burma Trading Corporation he built a bungalow on the sandy shore some twenty kilometres south-west of Karachi. Local usage fastened his name to the spot, and it remains on the map today as Hawke's Bay Beach — or, in the Pakistani vernacular, Hawksbay.

Hawke's Bay is today a gentle, family-friendly beach that draws hundreds of daily visitors for swimming, camel- and horse-riding, and weekend picnics. The beach huts that line the dune-back, first built by Karachi's commercial elite in the 1960s and 70s, trace their cultural origin to the sort of colonial seaside retreat that Hawke himself established. The bay is also ecologically significant: it is a protected nesting site for the green sea turtle and the olive ridley sea turtle, which come ashore during the winter months.

“The beach is named after Bladen Wilmer Hawke, who owned a house on the beach during the 1930s.”

Marriage & Seven Daughters

On 1 November 1934 Hawke married Ina Mary Faure Walker (c.1913–2002), the eldest daughter of Henry Faure Walker of Highley Manor, Balcombe, Sussex, and his wife Edith Ina Bartholomew. The couple had seven daughters and no sons. With no male heir the barony would, on Hawke's death in 1985, pass sideways to his younger brother Julian.

The Barony of Hawke

The barony of Hawke of Towton, in the County of York, was created in the Peerage of Great Britain on 20 May 1776. Its first holder was Admiral Sir Edward Hawke (1705–1781), victor of the Battle of Quiberon Bay on 20 November 1759 — a defeat so comprehensive that it ended France's threat of invading Britain during the Seven Years' War. The family motto, “Strike”, recalls the admiral's tactical reputation.

Arms, Crest & Supporters

The Hawke arms, as quartered by the Admiral's descendants, are blazoned: Quarterly, 1st and 4th, argent, a chevron erminois between three boatswain's whistles purpure (Hawke); 2nd and 3rd grand-quarter quarterly, or, a cross engrailed gules (Brooke), and argent, a chevron engrailed sable between three mullets sable (a Brooke heiress). The crest is a hawk rising ermine, beaked and belled, charged on the breast with a fleur-de-lis or. The supporters are a sea-green-mantled Neptune brandishing a sable trident (dexter) and a seahorse or, sustaining a broken banner (sinister) — recollections of the family's naval origins.

Documents, Records & Archives

The principal reference works covering Bladen Wilmer Hawke are the standard peerage volumes — Burke's, Debrett's and Cracroft's — together with the Wikipedia and Wikidata articles that synthesise them. The surviving documentary record is relatively modest: no public biography has been written of him, his parliamentary interventions as a whip and Lord-in-Waiting were necessarily anonymous, and his tenure at Bombay-Burma is documented chiefly through the Corporation's own archives (now held by the Tata-controlled successor firm BBTCL in Bombay) and scattered colonial administrative papers.
